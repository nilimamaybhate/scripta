2. LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1 Where It All Started
The idea of a machine writing like a human has been around for a long time. But for most of history, computer-generated text was easy to spot — it sounded robotic and unnatural. That changed when researchers introduced a new type of AI called the transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017). This technology became the engine behind tools like ChatGPT, which can now write essays, emails, and reports that sound completely human. Once these tools became available to everyone, a new problem appeared — how do you know if what you are reading was written by a person or a machine?
2.2 The First Attempts at Detection
The earliest detection methods looked at how predictable a piece of text is. AI tends to choose safe, expected words because it is trained to predict what comes next — making its writing smooth and statistically boring. Researchers found that this predictability, called perplexity, could be used as a detection signal (Solaiman et al., 2019). A tool called GLTR (Gehrmann et al., 2019) took this further by colour-coding each word based on how likely an AI was to choose it — which is very similar to the Token Heatmap feature in Scripta. These early approaches worked well at first but became less reliable as AI writing improved.
2.3 Using Smarter AI Models to Detect AI
Researchers soon realised that the best way to catch AI writing was to use AI itself. Models like BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and its smaller, faster cousin DistilBERT (Sanh et al., 2019) could be trained to tell the difference between human and machine text with much better accuracy than older methods. A major step forward came when researchers released the HC3 dataset (Guo et al., 2023) — a large collection of around 24,000 questions answered both by real humans and by ChatGPT. This gave developers a proper training resource for building detectors, and it is the same dataset used to train the model inside Scripta.
2.4 The Problems That Still Exist
Detection is not perfect. One study found that AI detectors often wrongly flag text written by people who are not native English speakers, marking it as AI-generated simply because the writing style is formal or different (Liang et al., 2023). This is a serious fairness issue, especially in schools. Another problem is paraphrasing — if someone takes AI-generated text and slightly rewrites it, most detectors stop working properly (Sadasivan et al., 2023; Krishna et al., 2023). These findings show that relying on just one signal or one model is not enough for reliable detection.
2.5 Making Detection Understandable
Several researchers have pointed out that a detection tool is only truly useful if it explains its reasoning. Getting a score of 87% AI means very little if you cannot see why. Work in explainable AI (Ribeiro et al., 2016) and interpretable detection methods like DetectGPT (Mitchell et al., 2023) has pushed the field toward showing users the evidence behind a verdict, not just the verdict itself. Scripta follows this direction by showing word-level heatmaps, sentence scores, and linguistic signals like burstiness and lexical diversity so users can see exactly what triggered the detection.
2.6 Summary
In short, the research shows that AI text detection has come a long way — from simple word statistics to powerful deep learning models. But challenges around fairness, paraphrasing, and explainability remain unsolved. Scripta is built with all of this in mind, combining a real trained model with multiple detection signals and a transparent interface that makes the results easy to understand and verify.
